02 / 08 The topic

Public, but not legible.

Company directors, officers, and 10%+ owners must file a Form 4 with the SEC within two business days of buying or selling their own stock. It is one of the few windows into what the people who know a company best are doing with their money.

The filings exist, but they are scattered, jargon-heavy, and unreadable to a non-expert. This tool turns that raw disclosure feed into something a general reader can search and understand.

03 / 08 The data · two sources

One scraped, one from an API — merged on ticker and date.

Source 1 — SEC EDGAR (scraped)

The "latest filings" feed of Form 4 disclosures. Gives me the insider, the company, the role, and the exact transactions: shares, price, buy or sell. The primary record, collected daily.

Source 2 — Yahoo Finance (API)

Daily closing price for each company. Answers the part Form 4 can't: was the insider's price a premium or a discount to what the public was paying that day?

The SEC filing is the ground truth of what happened; the price feed supplies the context that makes one trade meaningful to a general reader.

04 / 08 Process & challenges

A self-updating pipeline — and where it fought back.

Scrape

Pull the latest 100 Form 4s, then follow each into its XML for full transaction detail and footnote text.

Detect & log

A hash decides new vs. seen, so nothing is re-scraped. Each run writes a change log and error log.

Automate

A daily GitHub Action re-runs the scrape and commits new filings. 100 → 299 rows in three days.

  • 01The same filing appears twice — once under the insider, once under the company. 100 rows was really 42 filings, so I dedupe on the accession number.
  • 02Prices don't always match. Weekend/holiday trades have no close and tiny or foreign tickers aren't on Yahoo, so about 10% of trades get no market price.
  • 03A scoping question — Form 4 is a new dataset, so the stock-price source is what satisfies the two-source requirement.

07 / 08 Methodology & its limits

What this data is, and what it is not.

What it reveals

A near-real-time, verifiable record of insider activity. Every row links back to the original SEC filing, and filings are immutable once submitted.

Point of view

Collected by the insiders themselves under legal mandate — so it reflects a compliance lens, not an editorial one.

What is missing

Recent filings only, no deep history. Sales and grants dominate; open-market buys are rare. It records what was traded, never why.

Ethics

All public data, but the tool aggregates named individuals' finances. A trade is not proof of intent — the framing must not imply wrongdoing.
